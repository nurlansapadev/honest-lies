## ТАКТ 1 — HOOK

Teh-RAN. … A hot August morning. … Ten minutes to seven in the morning.

A Rockwell sedan — a Dodge — turns onto a side street near Voh-SOOG Square. A Volkswagen cuts in front. A minibus boxes it in from behind. Four men walk up on foot.

Inside: … three Americans. Engineers. Driving to work, like every morning.

A minute later, all three are dead. The Iranian driver takes a bullet. … By breakfast, the Americans are gone.

The official story is ready before the bodies are cold. Communist terrorists. Ideology. A blow against the friendship between Iran and America.

Clean. … Simple. … [sarcastic] Convenient.

And easy to believe — because Americans really were being killed nearby. The story isn't pulled from thin air.

One snag.

The man that same company hired to get to the bottom of it … didn't buy it for a second.

He dug. … He found.

And then it was very politely explained to him: … some things are better left buried.

[thoughtful] So who really killed three Americans — … and why was the man who dug it up told to keep quiet?

---

## ТАКТ 2 — OFFICIAL STORY

First — what were three American engineers doing in Teh-RAN at seven in the morning. … They were building EYE-becks.

Five hundred million dollars. … To feel the number: a couple of years earlier the Shah had dropped two billion dollars on eighty F-14 fighter jets — the biggest arms deal in American history at the time. EYE-becks ran a quarter of that. … A whole spying empire along the border — for the price of a good chunk of a fighter fleet.

A massive electronic-intelligence system: antennas, mountaintop stations, aircraft packed to the gills with gear. … Ears stretched along Iran's entire border — and that border ran right up against the Soviet Union.

The idea was simple and brazen. The Shah wanted to hear everything that moved around his country. The Americans wanted to get close to the Soviet missile ranges and listen to the Russians testing their hardware. … The interests lined up nicely. … [admiringly] I'll give them this — it was a bold piece of work. To sit on a mountain in Iran and eavesdrop on Moscow, you need nerve.

The contractor: Rockwell International. The same outfit that built America's spacecraft. The system got so complex they were shipping in the newest computers and cipher gear, and the people cleared to touch it carried C-I-A and N-S-A clearances.

And then death walks into the project — hundreds of millions of dollars, the sharp edge of the Cold War. … Three engineers, a side street, four gunmen.

The reaction was immediate. … The Shah himself explained what happened: a Communist plot. A blow to drive Iran and America apart and derail the country's military build-up.

And here's the thing — it sounded completely plausible. … Marxist militants had been operating in Teh-RAN for years. Three years earlier they'd shot an American colonel. Then two more. And that very summer, American intelligence noted that after a wave of arrests the militants had gone quiet — and shifted from well-guarded soldiers to softer targets. Business. Engineers. … There were eighteen thousand Americans in Iran back then.

So the story fit perfectly. Motive. Killers with a track record. Bodies. … SAH-vak — the Shah's secret police — took the case. The wounded driver was questioned. The picture came together.

Case closed. … [sarcastic] A little too neatly closed, if you ask me.

## ТАКТ 3 — TURN (разворот)

August twenty-eighth, nineteen seventy-six. … That's the official story.

Now — the man who wasn't buying it.

Gene Wheaton. A former Army criminal investigator. Rockwell brought him in as director of security — to clean things up after the murders.

Simple job: accept the story, plug the holes, move on.

Instead, Wheaton started asking questions nobody asked him to ask.

And what he found … looked nothing like the work of terrorists.

It looked like a cleanup.

---

## ТАКТ 4, часть 1 — REAL STORY: следователь входит

To understand why Wheaton didn't believe it, you have to understand who Wheaton was.

The man had already lived in Iran. In the early seventies he sat in Teh-RAN as an advisor — on narcotics and on terror — to the court of the Shah. He worked shoulder to shoulder with Iranian intelligence. He knew the country, knew the players, knew how things actually worked, not how they looked on paper.

Then he left the Army, came back as a civilian — and Rockwell put him on EYE-becks security. Right when three of his countrymen were gunned down on a side street.

And here's where I start to respect the man.

A good investigator doesn't ask "who's guilty." He asks "does the picture even add up." … Wheaton looked at the official story through the eyes of a man who'd spent the better part of twenty years catching people who lie convincingly.

And the picture didn't add up.

Look for yourself. Marxist militants kill for noise. Loud, public, meant to frighten. … This was different. The car boxed in front and back, textbook. Four men walked up, did the job, vanished. Clean. Planned. Military.

This wasn't the hysteria of ideologues. This was the work of people who knew the route, knew the time, knew who'd be in that car.

Somebody knew the three engineers' routine very well.

And you don't learn that from the street. … You learn that from the inside.

Wheaton pulled that thread. And it led him not outward — toward Iran's enemies.

It led inward. … Into the project itself.

---

## ТАКТ 4, часть 2 — REAL STORY: что внутри

So what did Wheaton see once he looked inside?

Money. … A great deal of money flowing the wrong way.

Five hundred million american BUCKS. Wherever that kind of money moves, a certain breed of people shows up. EYE-becks was no exception.

A few months after the murders, a Washington Post reporter took on the project — young, dogged, name of Woodward. The same one who'd brought down a president over Watergate. He dug into EYE-becks and described what he saw: middlemen with envelopes. Kickbacks. Contracts steered to the right people. Equipment so overcomplicated that plenty doubted it would ever work at all.

Five hundred million for a system that might never switch on. But the money was spent. The money was gone.

[admiringly] I'll grant it — the scale was something. The beauty of a scheme like this: nobody steals a fortune. Everybody just takes a little. And a little, times every hand in the chain, times five hundred million — adds up.

The C-I-A, for the record, denied all of it. It was preparing a paper for its director — no bribes, no secret payments, all clean. … Remember who sat in the director's chair at the C-I-A back then. We'll come back to him.

And how did the Shah himself wave off the whole business about faulty hardware? … Beautifully. Almost word for word: the talk, he said, came from "internal rivalry between some American factions."

"Internal rivalry between factions." … [sarcastic] Lovely. In plain English: their own people clawing at each other over who'd steal how much.

And when the theft runs that many floors deep — the top doesn't like it when someone at the bottom starts looking too closely.

And three engineers were close. … Very close to how EYE-becks actually worked.

---

## ТАКТ 4, часть 3 — REAL STORY: почему свои

Now the big question. If it wasn't terrorists — then who? … And what for?

Wheaton's theory — and I stress: this is Wheaton's read, his conclusion, not a court verdict — went like this.

The engineers weren't killed for being American. They were killed for being too close.

Think about who these three were. Not diplomats, not spies. Technical men. People who saw every day how the project really ran: what gear arrived, what didn't, what was paid for, and what existed only on paper. In a scheme where theft runs floor by floor, men like that are a risk. They don't have to go digging. It's enough to notice a discrepancy. … And mention it to the wrong person.

Wheaton believed they noticed.

And then the question "who hired the terrorists" turns into a much colder one: … who inside the project benefited from those three going quiet?

Wheaton started building a list. Not a list of terrorism suspects — a list of people on the inside with a history of handling other people's money. American contractors. Iranian officers with stars on their shoulders. Men with clearances and connections.

But where that list led said something about the scale. Wheaton wasn't digging up small fry. … He was digging up men with stars and offices.

And the higher you dig — the more interesting it gets, what happens to your report once it goes up.

And Wheaton's report went up.

---

## ТАКТ 4, часть 4 — REAL STORY: наверх и в тишину

When you're holding an investigation that says three Americans were killed by their own over money — what do you expect when you send it up the chain?

That somebody panics. Sends people. Starts shaking things loose.

So — that man I told you we'd get to. The one who ran the C-I-A that year. … George Bush. A future president. That year — head of intelligence.

And here's a detail worth knowing. The corruption around EYE-becks was being flagged upward before the shooting. A month before the murders, the American ambassador to Iran — Richard Helms, himself a former C-I-A director — sent Bush a handwritten note. Asked him to send people from the agency so they could hear the corruption allegations firsthand. … This isn't Wheaton's theory. This is a document.

And a month later, three engineers were gone.

And while Wheaton down below was building his list, up top the C-I-A was busy with something else. Preparing a paper for the director. Not to investigate the corruption charges — to refute them. To have an answer ready for the day Woodward printed his.

One man on the inside is trying to figure out who killed three colleagues. The apparatus up top is polishing the wording that everything's clean. … Two different jobs. The second one came through louder.

Wheaton's report set nothing in motion. No investigation, no arrests, no second look at the story. "Communist terrorists" stayed the official truth, just as before. And Wheaton — the man hired to get to the bottom of it — got thanked for getting to the bottom of it, and shown the door.

Now, straight, by the documents. There's no paper where Bush orders this buried. Wheaton never produced it under oath. … So the choice is simple. Either the top knew and looked away. … Or something duller and worse was at work: a machine that found it cheaper to swallow the terrorist story than to admit out loud that half a billion had slipped through its own people's fingers.

Which is worse — you decide.

Sometimes the truth isn't killed with a shot. … Sometimes it's just dropped in a bottom drawer. And pushed shut.

## ТАКТ 5 — FALLOUT (развязка)

Now — the bill. … How it all ended.

Start with the system itself. With half a billion dollars of ears along the Soviet border.

The Soviet Union didn't break them. No enemy service outplayed them. EYE-becks drowned in its own mess — in overcomplicated hardware, in kickbacks, in people who worked their own pockets harder than they worked anyone else's signals.

And then came seventy-nine.

The Shah, the man it was all built for, fled the country. Revolution. Ayatollahs. In that same Teh-RAN, a crowd stormed the American embassy and held fifty-two people hostage for more than a year.

Remember those eighty F-14s we opened with? America's finest hardware. It stayed parked on Iranian airfields — in the hands of a new regime that hated America to the bone. A system raised to guard Iran for its own — handed over, whole, to strangers.

So much for half a billion. … So much for national security.

Now — the three engineers. And let's use their names — they've earned that much.

Donald Smith. … Robert KRON-gard. … William COT-trel Junior. Three Americans, Rockwell engineers, driving to work that morning. When the car was boxed in and the guns opened up, Cottrell made it out of the car. … They killed him right there beside it.

The case of their murder officially stayed the work of terrorists. Nobody was tried for what it actually was. Wheaton's account — about their own, about money, about a cleanup — never made it into the record. For history, three men died at the hands of nameless Marxists, and that's that.

They had families. The families got the story. The truth — as far as Wheaton ever dug it up — nobody gave them.

And Wheaton himself?

Here's what I'll say. You can show a man the door. … Making him go quiet is a different job. Wheaton didn't go quiet. He carried his distrust of the official line further — and a few years later surfaced in another big story, where the same familiar faces turned up again wherever there was a whiff of money and gunpowder. But that, as they say, is another episode.

A clean plan. A beautiful system. … And a pile of bodies, money, and lies on the way out.

## ТАКТ 6 — TRANSLATION (перевод)

Let's translate a few words from official into plain English. … I do enjoy this.

"A Communist plot."

Sounds like a threat off a poster. But really — it was the best lid available. It explained the murder while explaining nothing. It pointed outward, at a convenient enemy, exactly when an honest finger would've pointed inward. … "A Communist plot" isn't the conclusion of an investigation. It's the end of one. The words after which people stop asking questions.

"Case closed."

Now this one's my favorite. Because it's true. Plain, notarized truth. The file really was closed — bound, numbered, sent to the archive. Nothing to argue with: case closed.

It's just that "closed" and "solved," as it turns out, are two very different words. To solve is to find who and why. To close is to stop looking and call it a result. … The case of three Americans was closed not because anyone reached the truth. It was closed because the truth cost more than silence.

That's how it's done. Nobody lies to your face — that's crude, it leaves marks. They tell you the plain truth: "case closed." And with that plain truth they cover everything underneath.

An honest word. … And an honest lie.

## ТАКТ 7 — BUTTON (финал)

Half a billion dollars to hear a foreign country a thousand miles off. … And three of their own, killed right under their nose, they never cared to hear.

Here's what gets me. They built a system to catch any whisper over the horizon — and built that same system so it wouldn't catch one inconvenient question at home.

The most expensive ears in the world. … And stone deaf, right when it mattered most.
