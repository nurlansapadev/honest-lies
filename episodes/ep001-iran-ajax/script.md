# Сценарий — Iran 1953 / Operation Ajax
*Длина: 20–30 минут | ~3200 слов*

---

## ТАКТ 1. ХУК (~0:00–1:00)

> Цель: разрыв любопытства + драматический вопрос. Без разгона.

In the summer of 1953, the people of Iran rose up. They poured into the streets, threw out an unstable prime minister who was dragging their country toward Moscow.

People welcomed their rightful king back to his throne. A proud nation saved itself from chaos.

With a little friendly help from its Western friends.

That's the story. That's the one they told for decades. Every word of it survived in the official history right up until the parts that didn't fit got quietly deleted.

Saved.

Hold on to that word. We're going to come back to it — after I tell you what it actually cost, and how the whole thing nearly fell apart in a basement in Tehran because a man back in Washington lost his nerve and one man on the ground refused to.

## ТАКТ 2. ОФИЦИАЛЬНАЯ ВЕРСИЯ (~1:00–3:30)

> Цель: установить принятую «правду». Ровный голос, чуть приподнятая бровь. Заложить эвфемизмы.

Here's how it was supposed to read.

By 1953, Iran had a problem, and his name was Mohammad Mossadegh. Prime minister. Elected, technically — and his admirers will never let you forget the "technically." A lawyer, a nationalist, a man who wept in parliament and governed in his pajamas from a sickbed. Charming, if you like that sort of thing. Washington did not.

The official telling goes like this. Mossadegh had seized the country's oil — nationalized it, took it right out of British hands — and run the economy into a ditch. He was ruling by emergency decree, dissolving parliament, gathering power into one pair of hands. And waiting in the wings, patient as winter, was the Tudeh — the Iranian Communist Party — and behind them, the Soviet Union, ready to swallow Iran whole and with it the oilfields and warm-water ports the West could not afford to lose.

So, the story says, the free world watched with concern. And when the Iranian people finally rose up against their failing prime minister, when they took to the streets demanding the return of their young Shah, the United States was simply there. Supportive. A friend lending a hand to a nation finding its own way back from the brink. Stability. Order. A communist catastrophe, averted.

That's the version. Restore order. Help the people. Save Iran. Three clean phrases. Remember all three — I'm going to hand each one back to you later, translated.

I'll give the storytellers this much: it's a tidy little tale. Hero, villain, a kingdom restored. The only trouble with it is the small matter of what actually happened.

## ТАКТ 3. РАЗВОРОТ (~30 секунд)

> Цель: один удар. Коротко.

The Iranian people didn't pick that summer to rise up. Somebody picked it for them. Somebody wrote the script, paid the crowd, printed the newspapers, and signed the checks.

And it wasn't an Iranian.

## ТАКТ 4. РЕАЛЬНАЯ ИСТОРИЯ (~10–14 минут)

> Цель: реальная операция, восторг к ремеслу, открытые петли, нарастание к кульминации.

Let's start where it really started — which is not with communism, and not with the Iranian people. It started with oil, and with the British, who were extremely fond of Iran's oil and extremely annoyed at having to share it.

For decades the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company — the outfit you know today as BP — pumped Iran's oil, paid Iran a humiliating slice of the profits, and kept the books in a language Iranians weren't allowed to audit. So in 1951, Mossadegh did the thing that sealed his fate: he nationalized it. He took his country's oil back. Wildly popular in Tehran. In London, it landed like a declaration of war.

The British wanted him gone. They tried it themselves, botched it, and got their whole spy network thrown out of the country for the trouble. So they went to Washington with a different sales pitch. They knew the Americans didn't care much about British oil profits. But communism — now *that* was a word that opened doors. Recast a stubborn nationalist as a Soviet stepping-stone, and suddenly it's not a corporate grudge, it's the free world in peril.

President Truman wasn't buying it. He said no. But Truman was on his way out, and the man coming in, Eisenhower, brought along two brothers who very much liked the idea: John Foster Dulles at the State Department, and his brother Allen at the CIA. The pitch worked. In the files, the operation got a cryptonym — TPAJAX. History would come to call it, simply, Operation Ajax. And it got a man.

That man was Kermit Roosevelt. Grandson of a president, which tells you everything about the circles he moved in. He slipped into Iran, set up shop, and got to work with a budget somewhere around a million dollars in cash — which in 1953 was the kind of money that could rent a great deal of a country.

Now — I'll give them this. The tradecraft was genuinely impressive. Cold, but impressive. They didn't send in tanks. They manufactured a reality.

They bought newspapers — not copies, *editors* — and filled them with stories painting Mossadegh as a communist, a Jew, an enemy of Islam, whatever stuck. They bought members of parliament. They bought street gangs and crowd leaders. And here's the move I have to tip my hat to, because it's pure poison and pure craft at the same time: they hired thugs to pose as communists. To riot in Mossadegh's name. To smash mosques and rough up clerics while shouting Tudeh slogans — so that respectable Iranians would look at the chaos in the streets and think, *my God, the communists are taking over, somebody stop this.*

Think about that. The threat they came to save Iran from — they were busy manufacturing it themselves, on a budget, with paid actors. That's not a coup. That's theater. Expensive, cynical, beautifully staged theater.

The plan itself was simple on paper. The Shah — young, nervous, more comfortable on a ski slope than a throne — would sign two royal decrees: one dismissing Mossadegh, one appointing the CIA's chosen replacement, a general named Fazlollah Zahedi. A general, I should mention, the British had locked up during the war for being a little too friendly with the Nazis. Standards were flexible.

There was just one problem. The Shah wouldn't sign. He dithered. He was terrified — terrified of his own prime minister, terrified it would fail, terrified of putting his name to it. Roosevelt had to send people to lean on him for weeks. Finally, finally, the Shah signed, and then did what frightened kings do: he packed his bags and left town, so he'd be safely abroad if it all went wrong.

Smart, as it turned out. Because it went wrong.

The night of August 15th, the colonel sent to arrest Mossadegh and deliver the decree showed up at his door — and got himself arrested instead. Mossadegh knew. He'd been tipped off. The whole carefully bought, beautifully staged operation collapsed in a single night. By morning, Tehran radio was announcing a failed coup attempt. The Shah, hearing the news in the region, panicked and fled — first to Baghdad, then to Rome, a king without a country, reportedly wondering aloud whether he'd ever go home.

And in Washington, they did what large nervous institutions always do when a plan goes sideways. They flinched. CIA headquarters sent the order down the wire: it's over. Cut your losses. Get out of Iran.

Which brings us to the basement.

Because Kermit Roosevelt read that cable — the official, signed order from his own bosses telling him to quit — and he decided, on his own authority, to ignore it. He stayed. He still had his network, his cash, his bought newspapers, and three more days of nerve than the men who'd sent him.

He flooded the streets with the decree — *look, the Shah legally dismissed Mossadegh, this is the law, not a coup.* He paid his crowds and turned them loose. He had his fake communists riot one more time to scare the public, then had his real crowds march in to "restore order." And this time he got the army to move with him.

On August 19th, the bought crowds and the turned soldiers converged on Mossadegh's home. There was a real battle — tanks, gunfire, hundreds dead before the day was out. Mossadegh climbed over his own garden wall and surrendered the next day. Zahedi, the CIA's general, was installed as prime minister. The Shah flew back from Rome to a throne the United States had just bought back for him.

Eleven days. One suitcase of cash. One man who refused to go home. And a government on the other side of the earth was simply... replaced. Like a part.

I have to be honest with you — as a piece of work, it's stunning. It nearly failed, it hung by a thread, it succeeded almost by accident and stubbornness. But it succeeded. And that, it turned out, was the worst thing that could have happened.

## ТАКТ 5. РАЗВЯЗКА (~3–5 минут)

> Цель: последствия. Цепь причин и следствий. Ирония. Без морализаторства.

Here's the thing nobody in Washington stopped to consider in the champagne afterglow: it had worked. And because it worked, everybody learned the wrong lesson.

Back home, the CIA had a triumph. Cheap, fast, deniable. You didn't need an army to change a government — you needed a man, a plan, and a suitcase. Overnight, covert regime change went from a wild idea to a tool in the standard kit. The very next year, the same playbook toppled the government of Guatemala. The template you're going to see again and again on this channel — it was poured, set, and stamped in Tehran in 1953.

In Iran itself, the bill came due slowly, with interest. The Shah came back, but he came back changed — and the country came back changed under him. He'd learned that he sat on his throne because a foreign power put him there, and he ruled like a man who never wanted to feel that helpless again. He built a secret police, the SAVAK, that became a byword for the knock on the door at night. The democratic opening Mossadegh represented — messy, flawed, real — was slammed shut, and stayed shut.

For twenty-five years the pressure built under the lid. And when it finally blew, in 1979, it didn't blow toward the gentle nationalism of a man in pajamas. It blew toward the mosque — toward the one institution the Shah's police couldn't fully crush. The revolution that swept him away was drenched in the memory of 1953, of the king the Americans bought back. When the students stormed the U.S. embassy in Tehran and took fifty-two Americans hostage for four hundred and forty-four days, they had a name for that embassy. They called it the "nest of spies." They weren't being paranoid. They'd read the history. They'd lived it.

Now — I want to be careful here, because the truth is a sharper tool than the legend. The coup did not *cause* the 1979 revolution the way a match causes a fire. Too much happened in twenty-six years to draw one clean line. But it built the room the fire started in. It put the Shah on the throne without a leash, shut the door on the moderate path, and taught a whole nation that their politics could be bought and sold by foreigners. You don't get the Iran of today — the hostility, the suspicion, the wall between Tehran and Washington — without the suitcase of cash in 1953. We didn't light the fire. We just spent twenty-six years building the room and filling it with kindling.

## ТАКТ 6. ПЕРЕВОД (~1–2 минуты)

> Цель: вернуться к посеянным словам-крючкам, расшифровать эвфемизмы.

Remember those three clean phrases from the official story? I promised you translations. Here they are.

"Restore order." The order they restored was a king who answered to no one and a secret police that answered to him. Order, the way a clenched fist is orderly.

"Help the people." The people, you'll recall, had elected the man we removed. We didn't help them get what they wanted. We helped them get what *we* wanted, and called it the same thing.

And "save Iran." Save it from what — communism? The CIA's own analysts had reported the communists couldn't take power. Save it for whom, is the better question. The answer is oil, and a friendly throne, and a Cold War scoreboard. We didn't save Iran. We took it off the board and put our own piece down.

One last word. "Saved." I told you to hold on to it.

## ТАКТ 7. ФИНАЛ (~30–60 секунд)

> Цель: один точный удар. Каламбур «тогда против сейчас» или тихая забота. Сказал — и тишина.

In 1953, it took us eleven days and one suitcase of money to reach across the entire planet and pick a country's government for it. Eleven days.

We've spent the seventy years since finding out what that costs.

Saved. Yeah.

Some rescues, you keep paying for.
